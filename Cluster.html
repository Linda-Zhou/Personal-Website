Clustering analysis of Multi-omics data

Why did we perform clustering analysis?

Univariate associations of metabolites and proteins with adverse health outcomes have identified a few promising biomarkers. However, many of these biomarkers are correlated, and an integrated analysis of both metabolomic and proteomic platforms may better elucidate deregulated pathways.

Which clustering algorithm did we use?

We used Netboost because this algorithm could identify weak but cumulative effects of features found in many high-dimensional omics datasets, which could play an important role in certain diseases.[Read more]

What data did we use?

We used The Atherosclerosis Risk in Communities (ARIC) study as discovery dataset and the African American Study of Kidney Disease and Hypertension (AASK) as validation dataset. ARIC is an ongoing longitudinal cohort of 15,792 middle-aged men and women recruited from four communities in the U.S. since 1987, the AASK study is a cohort of 694 adult African Americans aged 18-70 years with hypertensive chronic kidney disease recruited from 21 clinical centers in the United States. We developed clusters in the ARIC study and transfer the cluster membership to the AASK study.

What did we find from this study?

We identified 224 clusters of correlated proteins and metabolites in the ARIC study. These 224 clusters identified in ARIC had similar within-module correlation structures in AASK, indicating good transferability (spearman correlation of 0.84). There were 52 modules associated with mortality in ARIC (p < 0.05/224), of which seven were significantly associated AASK (P < 0.05/52). Through a literature review we linked these seven modules to distinct pathways associated with heart disease, kidney function, and diabetes.
